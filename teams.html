American League

The American League of Professional Baseball Clubs, known simply as the American League (AL), is one of two leagues that make up Major League Baseball. It developed from the Western League, a minor league based in the Great Lakes states, which eventually aspired to major league status. It is sometimes called the Junior Circuit because it claimed Major League status for the 1901 season, 25 years after the formation of the National League (the "Senior Circuit").

National League

The National League of Professional Baseball Clubs, known simply as the National League (NL), is the older of two leagues constituting Major League Baseball (MLB) in the United States and Canada, and the world's oldest extant professional team sports league. Minor League Teams

Minor League Baseball (MiLB) serves as a developmental league for Major League Baseball (MLB) teams. It consists of numerous leagues and levels spread across the United States and Canada. The primary purpose of the minor leagues is to nurture young talent, providing players opportunities to develop their skills before potentially advancing to the major leagues.

MiLB is structured into various levels or classifications, often referred to as "farm teams" or affiliates, which align with MLB clubs. These levels include Triple-A, Double-A, High-A, Single-A (often divided into two sub-levels: Advanced-A and Low-A), and Rookie leagues. Players typically start their professional careers in Rookie or Single-A levels and progress upwards based on performance and potential.

Each MLB team has affiliations with minor league clubs, where they send their prospects to gain experience and refine their skills. Minor league teams serve as training grounds for players, coaches, and umpires, offering a competitive environment to prepare for the demands of Major League Baseball.

Rookie

Rookie leagues in Minor League Baseball (MiLB) cater to newly drafted or signed players, typically straight out of high school or college. These leagues serve as an introductory platform for players to acclimate to professional baseball, emphasizing skill development, game adaptation, and adjusting to the demands of the sport. Rookie leagues often focus on providing a supportive environment for young talent to grow and progress within the minor league system.

Single-A

Single-A in Minor League Baseball (MiLB) encompasses two sub-levels: High-A and Low-A, serving as initial stages for player development. It acts as a starting point for young prospects entering professional baseball, focusing on skill enhancement, adapting to higher competition, and preparing players for advancement through the minor league system. Single-A teams are affiliated with Major League Baseball clubs, providing a foundational platform for players to begin their professional careers and progress toward higher levels within the minor leagues.

Double-A

Double-A in Minor League Baseball (MiLB) represents a pivotal stage for player development, positioned between Single-A and Triple-A. It serves as a significant step up in competition and skill level, where prospects face more experienced opponents and refine their abilities for potential advancement. Double-A teams consist of promising players honing their skills, aiming to catch the attention of Major League Baseball clubs for potential call-ups to the big leagues.

Triple-A

Triple-A, the highest level in Minor League Baseball (MiLB), serves as a crucial bridge between the minors and the Major Leagues. It features experienced players on the cusp of reaching or returning to the MLB. Triple-A teams often consist of prospects awaiting a call-up to the majors, seasoned players aiming for a return, and those refining their skills for potential contributions at the highest level of baseball.
