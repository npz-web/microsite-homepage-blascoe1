The History of Baseball

Discover the rich history, exciting gameplay, and fascinating stories that make baseball an integral part of American culture. From the early days of the sport to the modern era, we delve deep into its evolution, legendary players, and iconic moments.



Babe's Called Shot
1932

It was Oct. 1, 1932, the New York Yankees vs. the Chicago Cubs in Game 3 of the 1932 Fall Classic.

During the fifth inning of the game at Wrigley Field, Babe Ruth was at the plate facing pitcher Charlie Root. Ruth, amid heckling and taunts from the Cubs players and fans, pointed towards the outfield, either gesturing or verbally calling his shot, indicating that he would hit a home run to that exact spot. On the next pitch, Ruth slammed a home run to deep center field. As he rounded the bases, it appeared that he pointed to the outfield, possibly reinforcing the claim that he had indeed called his shot.

However, there's controversy surrounding this moment, with some witnesses and even Ruth himself downplaying the gesture, stating that Ruth was merely responding to the taunting from the Cubs' bench, rather than specifically calling his shot.

Regardless of the exact truth, the "called shot" has become a part of baseball folklore, symbolizing Ruth's confidence and larger-than-life persona. It remains a debated and celebrated moment in baseball history, contributing to the mystique surrounding one of the game's greatest players.

The Luckiest Man Alive
1939

Lou Gehrig, famed for his 2,130 consecutive-games-played streak, made one of the most memorable speeches in the annals of sports. It was on July 4, 1939, now Lou Gehrig Appreciation Day, when the longtime Yankee first baseman uttered the famous words at a home plate ceremony at Yankee Stadium: “For the past two weeks you have been reading about a bad break. Yet today I consider myself the luckiest man on the face of the earth.”

Gehrig had been forced to retire as a player two weeks earlier due to his being diagnosed with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, the disease that today bears his name. Gehrig died at the age of 37 on June 2, 1941, but he lives on through his legacy as "the Iron Horse" of baseball.

The Rise of Jackie Robinson
1947

Jackie Robinson signed with the Brooklyn Dodgers on April 10th, 1947, and he was the first African American to play in a major league team, breaking the race barrier in baseball. He was praised for his athleticism on the field, being named Rookie of the Year in 1947 and getting inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame in 1962.

Robinson was the first professional athlete to have his jersey number, 42, retired across the sport. The number 42 is now worn by all baseball players in the league during games held on April 15th, the date of Robinson’s MLB debut, which is now known as Jackie Robinson Day.

New All-Time Hits Record
1985

September 11, 1985, was a historic day for baseball as Cincinnati Reds player Pete Rose broke Ty Cobb's all-time hit record of 4,191 hits. Rose amassed an incredible 4,256 hits, a record that still stands today.

Cubs Break the Curse
2016

The Chicago Cubs had not won a World Series since 1908. Known as the Curse of the Billy Goat after an incident in 1945 when Billy Goat Tavern owner William Sianis was asked to leave the stadium after his pet goat, named Murphy, was bothering other fans. Outraged, Sianis allegedly declared, "Them Cubs, they ain't gonna win no more".

The supposed curse lasted 71 years, from 1945 to the Cubs 2016 championship season. In 2016, the Cubs, led by a talented roster that included Kris Bryant, Anthony Rizzo, and Jake Arrieta, battled their way through the playoffs and found themselves facing off against the Cleveland Indians in the World Series. After the incident with Sianis and Murphy, the supposed curse lasted 71 years, from 1945 to the Cubs 2016 Championship season.

On the 46th anniversary of William Sianis' death, the "curse" was broken when they defeated the Los Angeles Dodgers 5–0 in Game 6 of the 2016 National League Championship Series to win the NL pennant. Later that season, the Cubs then went on to secure the World Series victory against the American League champion Cleveland Indians. With a score of 8–7 in 10 innings in Game 7, they secured victory 108 years after their last win.
